Privacy

This isn't a legal document — it's a plain description of what CheckMyARM actually does with your data today, based directly on how the app is built. See About for how compatibility classification works.

What we collect

When you run a scan, the scanner sends your browser (and then our server) a list of your installed applications, some basic device information, and the compatibility results we look up for them:

  • Installed applications — name, publisher, and version, so we can match them against our compatibility database.
  • Basic device information — device names and types (like "camera" or "printer"), used the same way.
  • Compatibility data — the native/emulated/unsupported verdicts we already know, matched to what you have installed.

Sharing your own compatibility findings with the community is optional, and signing in with Google or GitHub is optional too — CheckMyARM works fully anonymously if you never do either.

What we do NOT collect

CheckMyARM currently:

  • does not request your email address from Google or GitHub when you sign in
  • does not store the OAuth access or refresh tokens Google/GitHub issue during sign-in
  • does not use analytics of any kind
  • does not use advertising trackers
  • does not use marketing pixels
  • does not retain raw device serial numbers or full Plug-and-Play identifiers — only a generic vendor/product code (like "this is a Logitech webcam"), never anything that identifies your specific physical unit

Scan retention

Scan sessions are temporary. They exist primarily so we can generate your report and let you refresh it later without re-scanning — not as a permanent record of your machine.

Compatibility contributions are different: if you share what you found about an app, that contribution is intentionally kept, because it becomes part of the shared compatibility database that helps everyone else's reports.

We'll be upfront that we don't yet have a way for you to export or delete your data on your own. That's a real gap, and something we intend to address in a future update.

Community contributions

You can share what you found about an app's ARM compatibility without signing in — we recognize your browser with a cookie so you don't end up voting on the same app twice, not by anything tied to your identity.

Signing in is entirely optional, and never required to use the scanner or contribute. If you sign in after already contributing anonymously, your prior contributions move to your account automatically.

If you change your mind about an app, resubmitting updates your existing answer in place — it doesn't create a second, conflicting entry.

Third-party services

CheckMyARM relies on a small number of outside services, only where the app actually needs them:

  • Google OAuth — only if you choose to sign in with Google.
  • GitHub OAuth — only if you choose to sign in with GitHub.
  • GitHub compatibility lookups — used to help classify less common apps by checking for an ARM64 release on GitHub.
  • Railway — the hosting platform that runs CheckMyARM.

Our philosophy

We believe compatibility information is valuable, but personal inventories are not. CheckMyARM tries to collect only what's needed to generate your report and improve the shared compatibility database — nothing more.
