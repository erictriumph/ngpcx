About CheckMyARM™

CheckMyARM began with a simple question.

How can someone know whether their existing software and devices will work — before investing in a Windows on ARM PC?

At the time, the alternatives were hours of manual research, digging through forum posts, or simply hoping for the best. There had to be a better way.

CheckMyARM was built to make that process fast, objective, and a lot less uncertain — a single scan of the PC you already own, checked against a compatibility database that keeps getting better.

How it works

CheckMyARM maintains an evolving, evidence-based compatibility database. Applications without verified compatibility data are marked Unknown rather than assumed broken — most conventional x86/x64 Windows applications are expected to run under emulation until confirmed native or unsupported.

The scanner reads what's installed on your machine, checks it against that database, and gives you a plain-language report — not a guess, and not a sales pitch.

Community

No single source can catalog every application. When CheckMyARM doesn't have an answer yet, real people fill the gap — anonymously or signed in, it's entirely optional either way. A small group of trusted Researcher volunteers helps investigate the harder cases and keep existing entries accurate over time.

Want to dig deeper into why compatibility works the way it does? See the Why page.

Privacy

The scanner talks to your browser over localhost:7878 — that connection never leaves your machine. All real network traffic (submitting scan results, checking compatibility data) goes over HTTPS on port 443 to the CheckMyARM server, the same as any normal website.

Open philosophy

CheckMyARM's compatibility research is done by hand — by admins and volunteer Researchers using the same public search results anyone could find — not scraped in bulk or bought from a paid API. It's slower, but it keeps the data honest.

One bit of trivia in the spirit of that honesty: CheckMyARM runs on a codebase we still internally call NGPCX (Next-Gen PC eXplorer) — we just like the name CheckMyARM better for everyone else.
